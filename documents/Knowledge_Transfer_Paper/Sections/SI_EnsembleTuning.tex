\label{appendix:ensemble_tuning}

\rev{The ensemble size $n$ is a critical hyperparameter of the EnKF: too few members under-sample the prior and introduce spurious correlations, while an unnecessarily large ensemble increases computational cost without meaningful accuracy gains. To select an appropriate ensemble size for each cell line group, a systematic convergence study was performed by running the full dual estimation for every dataset at ensemble sizes $n \in \{10, 25, 50, 75\}$ and evaluating two complementary error metrics alongside wall-clock runtime.}

\rev{The pointwise absolute error between the EnKF state estimate and the experimental measurement at each measurement time $t_k$ for state $j$ is}
\rev{
\begin{equation}
e_{k,j} = \bigl|\hat{x}_{k,j} - y_{k,j}\bigr|
\label{eq:pointwise_ae}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{x}_{k,j}$ is the EnKF posterior mean for state $j$ at time $t_k$ and $y_{k,j}$ is the corresponding experimental measurement.}

\rev{To compare across state variables with different magnitudes and units, each pointwise error is normalised by a state-specific scale factor $\tilde{e}_j$, defined as the median absolute error of state $j$ across all ensemble sizes:}
\rev{
\begin{equation}
\tilde{e}_j = \mathrm{median}_{k,\,n}\!\bigl(e_{k,j}\bigr)
\label{eq:mae_scale}
\end{equation}
This prevents high magnitude states (e.g.\ viable cell density) from dominating the aggregate score.}

\rev{The \textbf{overall normalised MAE} is then computed by averaging the normalised errors across all $n_t$ measurement time points and $n_x$ state variables:}
\rev{
\begin{equation}
\overline{\mathrm{MAE}}_{\mathrm{norm}} = \frac{1}{n_t}\sum_{k=1}^{n_t}\frac{1}{n_x}\sum_{j=1}^{n_x}\frac{e_{k,j}}{\tilde{e}_j}
\label{eq:norm_mae}
\end{equation}
A lower value indicates better overall accuracy.}

\rev{The normalised MAE at individual measurement days may vary considerably: estimation accuracy can be high on certain days and low on others. To assess whether the estimator performs consistently across the culture rather than merely achieving a favourable average, the \textbf{standard deviation of normalised MAE} is computed over measurement time points:}
\rev{
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{\mathrm{norm}} = \mathrm{std}_{k}\!\left(\frac{1}{n_x}\sum_{j=1}^{n_x}\frac{e_{k,j}}{\tilde{e}_j}\right)
\label{eq:std_mae}
\end{equation}
A low $\sigma_{\mathrm{norm}}$ indicates that estimation accuracy is uniform across all measurement days, whereas a high value suggests that the overall MAE is driven by strong performance on a subset of days while other days exhibit substantially larger errors. An ensemble size is therefore selected only when both $\overline{\mathrm{MAE}}_{\mathrm{norm}}$ and $\sigma_{\mathrm{norm}}$ are simultaneously low.}

\rev{Figure~\ref{fig:ensemble_tuning} summarises the results. For the three \textbf{Cell Line~A} datasets (panels a--c), the overall normalised MAE reaches a minimum at $n = 50$; increasing to $n = 75$ yields no statistically meaningful improvement while the runtime approximately doubles. An ensemble size of 50 was therefore selected for all Cell Line~A conditions.}

\rev{For \textbf{Cell Line~B}, the datasets differ from the base model calibration cell line in cell line, product, and feeding strategy. For Feed~C (panel~d) and Feed~U (panel~e), the normalised MAE continues to decrease from $n = 50$ to $n = 75$, indicating that the larger ensemble provides additional benefit when the model--data mismatch is substantial. For Feed~U~+~40\% (panel~f), $n = 50$ and $n = 75$ show comparable accuracy; nevertheless, $n = 75$ was adopted uniformly across all three Cell Line~B datasets to ensure a consistent comparison across feeding strategies and to provide a more conservative uncertainty representation when transferring to a new cell line.}

\rev{\textbf{Computational cost.} All simulations were performed in Python~3.14 on a desktop workstation equipped with a 13th-generation Intel Core i5-13600K processor (14 cores, 20 threads, 3.5~GHz base clock) and 32~GB DDR4 RAM, running Windows~11 (64-bit). The mechanistic model was integrated using SciPy's LSODA solver (\texttt{scipy.integrate.ode}, LSODA) with a time step of 0.01~h ($\approx$36~s). With the selected ensemble sizes ($n = 50$ for Cell Line~A, $n = 75$ for Cell Line~B), a complete simulation of the full fed-batch culture (12--14 days, 12--14 measurement updates) required approximately 2--3 minutes of wall-clock time. This runtime is well within the timescales of practical interest for fed-batch monitoring, where off-line measurements are typically available every 24~h, confirming the feasibility of the framework for near-real-time state estimation and decision support in manufacturing environments.}
